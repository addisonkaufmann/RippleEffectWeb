There are approximately 24,183 abandoned mines in Arizona.
(Bureau of Land Management, 2015)

Reclamation of abandoned mines to a pre-mining state of land is difficult, if not impossible, and when done, it is often a long, challenging, and expensive process.

The US EPA Office of Inspector General identified 156 “mega” hard rock mining sites nationwide that could cost $24 billion to clean up and maintain, a cost more than 12 times the annual US EPA budget for remediation of large hazardous waste (i.e., Superfund) sites.
(Lovingood et al., 2004)

Potential point sources of pollution in Arizona include industrial waste, underground storage tanks, landfills, mines, and wastewater treatment plants. These activities are specifically regulated and monitored through programs operated by ADEQ.

Arizona Department of Environmental Quality’s (ADEQ) 20 Year Groundwater Study
Produced by Douglas Towne and Jason Jones
ADEQ conducted a study of groundwater quality in Arizona communities adjacent to hazardous waste sites. This study has found 1/3 of these wells contain one or more contaminants at unsafe levels - levels that exceed the federal maximum contaminant level (MCL) for drinking water set by the EPA. All of the contaminants measured are known to be harmful to human health depending on exposure and concentration. These substances can be invisible, odorless, and tasteless, so people can unknowingly threaten their health for years.

More than 330,000 Arizonians, or 5% of the state’s population, use a private well for drinking water.

In an effort to characterize groundwater quality, samples were collected from 39 of Arizona’s 51 groundwater basins between 1995 and 2015.

Of the 1,766 sites sampled, 35% exceeded at least one health-based water quality standard (Primary MCL’s).

Exceedences of the Primary MCL:
22% Arsenic
11% Fluoride
10% Nitrate
16% Gross Alpha/Uranium

Production wells used for domestic, stock, irrigation, industrial, and public water supply were sampled for the studies.

Groundwater constitutes about 3.1 million acre feet or 43 % of Arizona’s annual water use. The vast majority of groundwater pumped in the state is used to irrigate crops and for public water supplies.
